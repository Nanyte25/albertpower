Church & Religious Works

Devotional sculpture across Ireland

Alongside his revolutionary portraits and public memorials, Albert G. Power produced a substantial body of ecclesiastical and devotional work for churches, cathedrals, colleges, and religious institutions across Ireland. These commissions — in Portland stone, limestone, marble, and granite — represent a quieter, more contemplative dimension of his practice, and many remain in situ in buildings open to visitors today.

Power was a devout Catholic whose nationalist and religious convictions were inseparable. He spoke publicly at the Academy of Christian Art in 1936 about his approach to devotional sculpture, arguing that Irish artists should use native materials and seek subjects rooted in Irish spiritual life.

Dublin — Glasnevin Cemetery

c.1940

Memorial to John and Mary McKernan

Limestone · Glasnevin Cemetery, Dublin

A major funerary monument commissioned by Fr John McKernan, a chaplain at University College Dublin, in memory of his parents John and Mary McKernan (d. 1937). The work is among Power's most ambitious cemetery commissions — a tall limestone cross monument with a carved crucified Christ on the upper shaft and figural relief panels depicting sacred scenes on the lower sections. The carving of the Christ figure is particularly notable: the crown of thorns, the INRI inscription, and the radiating halo are handled with considerable sculptural power.

Source: Síghle Bhreathnach-Lynch, Expressions of Nationhood in Bronze & Stone. Photographs © Brian Lynch.

1920s–1940s

Further Monuments — Glasnevin Cemetery

Limestone and bronze · Glasnevin Cemetery, Dublin

Power produced a number of funerary monuments for Glasnevin over several decades. Confirmed commissions include the Downes Monument (1925), the Archbishop Walsh Tomb (1929), the Cait O'Kelly Memorial (1936), and the McKernan Memorial (c.1940). Glasnevin's landscape of nationalist monuments made it a natural home for Power's work — many of the figures he commemorated in portrait busts also have graves here.

Dublin — Whitechurch Cemetery

1930s

Memorial to Mary Alicia Trench

Limestone · Whitechurch Cemetery, Rathfarnham, Co. Dublin

A freestanding limestone memorial stele to Mary Alicia Trench (d. 3 February 1930), wife of Wilbraham Fitzjohn Trench, in the old Church of Ireland graveyard at Whitechurch, Rathfarnham. The memorial is carved with a rising sun motif at the top — referencing the inscription "unto you that fear my name shall the Sun of righteousness arise with healing in his wings" — and a stylised tree or plant form down the shaft. The lower portion records Wilbraham Fitzjohn Trench himself. The work is characteristic of Power's ability to move between nationalist Catholic commissions and work for Protestant patrons.

Source: Síghle Bhreathnach-Lynch, Expressions of Nationhood in Bronze & Stone. Photograph © Síghle Bhreathnach-Lynch.

Dublin — Churches & Colleges

c.1936

Madonna and Child

All Hallows College, Grace Park Road, Drumcondra, Dublin

Power displayed photographs of this Madonna and Child at his 1936 talk to the Academy of Christian Art as a key example of his religious work, alongside his secular busts and public monuments. Date of original commission unconfirmed. All Hallows College is now Dublin City University's All Hallows campus.

Co. Dublin — Skerries

1939

Statue of St Patrick — the hidden goat

Portland stone · St Patrick's Church, Skerries, Co. Dublin

Carved in situ on a scaffold on the exterior façade of the new St Patrick's Church, Skerries, designed by architect J.J. Robinson and consecrated in 1939. Power argued for Skerries limestone but Portland stone was chosen as cheaper.

Power discovered that local legend held St Patrick had visited Skerries and that someone had stolen one of his goats — giving rise to the centuries-old taunt "Skerry Goats." He secretly carved a goat's head projecting from the Celtic interlace base of the statue. After the church opened, the Archbishop of Dublin Edward Byrne instructed his secretary Fr Dunne to write demanding removal. The goat remains to this day. A separate bronze plaque commemorating the goat was later added to the church in 1989.

Co. Donegal — Carndonagh

1943–1945

Sacred Heart Church, Carndonagh — Four works

Granite · Sacred Heart Church, Carndonagh, Co. Donegal

Power's last major commission, executed in the final two years of his life. He carved four figures in granite for the Sacred Heart Church on the Inishowen Peninsula — one of the oldest Christian sites in Ireland, deeply associated with St Patrick's mission to the north. The three patron saints of Ireland stand at the corners of the church dome; the Sacred Heart figure was the last work photographed in progress in his Berkeley Road yard before his death in July 1945.

  • St Patrick, 1943–5, granite — dome
  • St Brigid, 1943–5, granite — dome
  • St Colmcille, 1943–5, granite — dome
  • Statue of the Sacred Heart, 1945, stone

Source: Judith Hill monograph; photograph in The Standard, 1945.

Co. Galway

1941

St Joseph and Christ Child

Limestone · St Joseph's College, Garbally, Ballinasloe, Co. Galway

A limestone grouping of St Joseph with the Christ Child for St Joseph's College, Garbally — a diocesan college run by the Diocese of Clonfert near Ballinasloe. One of two versions Power made of this subject; he also produced an earlier marble version for Foxford, Co. Mayo (late 1920s).

Co. Kerry

1939

Pikeman

Limestone · Denny Street, Tralee, Co. Kerry

A full-length figure of a pikeman commemorating the 1798 rebellion. Power replaced the original centenary statue toppled by the Black and Tans in 1921. In Power's own words, the figure placed on tree roots symbolises: "this man represents his country, the man who had lost everything. His house has been burnt, everything has been taken away from him. There is nothing remaining to him but the roots of his trees. Yet he is standing on the roots which support his claim to right and justice and faces life sure of conquering. This man is Ireland." The plinth panels commemorate the uprisings of 1798, 1803, 1848 and 1867.

Co. Sligo

1937

Statue of Bishop Laurence Gillooly

Limestone · Gillooly Hall, Temple Street, Sligo

Full-length figure of Bishop Laurence Gillooly (1858–1895), Bishop of Elphin, on the Community Hall opposite St Mary's Cathedral, Sligo. Completed April 1937, unveiled May 1937 by Dr Edward Dooly, the resident Bishop of Elphin. Power worked from photographs and oil paintings; the crozier was carved from a replica owned by the deceased.
