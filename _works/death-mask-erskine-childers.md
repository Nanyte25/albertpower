Robert Erskine Childers — author, sailor, and passionate Irish republican — was executed by firing squad on 24 November 1922, at Beggars Bush Barracks, Dublin, on the order of the Provisional Government he had opposed. He was forty-two years old. His execution was one of the most controversial acts of the Civil War period, carried out just weeks after the government had introduced emergency powers allowing execution for possession of arms.

Family tradition holds that Albert Power made a death mask of Childers in the aftermath of his execution, adding him to the sequence of masks he made of Cathal Brugha, Arthur Griffith, and Michael Collins in the same year. **This claim is not yet independently confirmed.** No photograph of an Albert Power death mask of the elder Erskine Childers has been located in the family archive, and a search of National Museum of Ireland and other public collection records did not turn up a corroborating record.

<aside class="source-note" style="font-size:0.85em;color:#888;font-style:italic;margin-top:1em;">
<strong>Research note:</strong> A photograph previously used to illustrate this page — showing a man finishing a bronze bust, captioned "James Power with his portrait of former President, Erskine Childers" — was discovered to depict a different work entirely: James Power's (Albert's son) memorial bust of <strong>President Erskine Childers</strong>, the son of this Erskine Childers, who served as the fourth President of Ireland (1973–74) and died in 1974. That work is correctly documented on the <a href="{{ '/connections/james-power-works/' | relative_url }}">James Power Works</a> page. The two men share a name, which appears to have caused the photograph to be misattributed to this entry in an earlier version of this site. This page is flagged pending further verification of whether Albert Power's 1922 death mask of the elder Erskine Childers genuinely exists.
</aside>

**Significance, if confirmed:** would form part of the most concentrated sequence of revolutionary death masks ever made in Ireland — four major figures of the independence movement, all within months of each other in 1922.

*Erskine Childers's son, also named Erskine Childers, later became the fourth President of Ireland (1973–1974) — a separate person from the subject of this entry.*
